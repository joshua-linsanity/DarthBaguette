
 **Claim (Part b).** Suppose $(x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3})$ and $(y_{1}, y_{2}, y_{3})$ are two pairs of three distinct points on the real axis, with $x_{1} < x_{2} < x_{3}$ and $y_{1} < y_{2} < y_{3}$. There exists a (unique) automorphism $\Phi$ of $\mathbb{H}$ so that $\Phi(x_{j}) = y_{j}$, for $j=1,2,3$. The same conclusion holds if $y_{3} < y_{1} < y_{2}$ or $y_{2} < y_{3} < y_{1}$. *Proof.* Define the following: $$ a \equiv \frac{{x_1} {y_1} ({y_2}-{y_3})+{x_3} ({y_1}-{y_2}) {y_3}+{x_2} {y_2} (-{y_1}+{y_3})}{\sqrt{({x_1}-{x_2}) ({x_1}-{x_3}) ({x_2}-{x_3}) ({y_1}-{y_2}) ({y_1}-{y_3}) ({y_2}-{y_3})}} $$ and then $$ b \equiv \frac{{x_2} {x_3} {y_1} ({y_2}-{y_3})+{x_1} {x_2} ({y_1}-{y_2}) {y_3}+{x_1} {x_3} {y_2} (-{y_1}+{y_3})}{\sqrt{({x_1}-{x_2}) ({x_1}-{x_3}) ({x_2}-{x_3}) ({y_1}-{y_2}) ({y_1}-{y_3}) ({y_2}-{y_3})}} $$ and then  $$ c\equiv\frac{{x_3} ({y_1}-{y_2})+{x_1} ({y_2}-{y_3})+{x_2} (-{y_1}+{y_3})}{\sqrt{({x_1}-{x_2}) ({x_1}-{x_3}) ({x_2}-{x_3}) ({y_1}-{y_2}) ({y_1}-{y_3}) ({y_2}-{y_3})}} $$ and finally $$ d \equiv \frac{{x_1} {x_2} ({y_1}-{y_2})+{x_2} {x_3} ({y_2}-{y_3})+{x_1} {x_3} (-{y_1}+{y_3})}{\sqrt{({x_1}-{x_2}) ({x_1}-{x_3}) ({x_2}-{x_3}) ({y_1}-{y_2}) ({y_1}-{y_3}) ({y_2}-{y_3})}} $$ Observe that when $y_{1} < y_{2} < y_{3}$, or a cyclic permutation thereof (i.e. the other permutations considered in the problem), we have that $a,b,c,d\in \mathbb{R}$ since the term under the square root is positive. Moreover, it can be observed that $ad - bc = 1$; indeed, the denominators are all identical and hence subtracting the product of the numerators of $a$ and $d$ with those of $b$ and $c$ yields precisely the term under the square root. Now consider the map $$ \Phi(z) = \frac{az+b}{cz+d}, \quad \Phi : \mathbb{H} \to \mathbb{H} $$ which is an automorphism by Theorem 2.4. Observe $\Phi(x_{j})=y_{j}$ for all $j=1,2,3$. Then note that if $\phi_{1}, \phi_{2}$ are two automorphisms of $\mathbb{H}$ such that both maps send $x_{j}$ to $y_{j}$ for all $j=1,2,3$, then $\psi\equiv\phi_{1}^{-1}\circ\phi_{2}: \mathbb{H}\to \mathbb{H}$ satisfies $\psi(x_{j})=x_{j}$ for all $j=1,2,3$, so that by Part (a), we have that $\psi=\text{id}$ and therefore $\phi_{1}=\phi_{2}$. It follows that such maps are unique, and hence we have that $\Phi$ is unique. $\square$
